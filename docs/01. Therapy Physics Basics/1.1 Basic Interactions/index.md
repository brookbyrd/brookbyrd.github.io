# Basic Interactions

## Learning Goals

By the end of this section, you should be able to:

- Describe the major photon interactions relevant to radiation therapy.
- Explain how interaction probability depends on energy and atomic number.
- Connect photon interactions to attenuation, dose deposition, image contrast, and shielding.
- Distinguish photoelectric effect, Compton scatter, and pair production.

---

## Big Picture

Radiation therapy beams interact with matter through a small number of dominant physical processes. For megavoltage photon therapy, the most important interaction is usually **Compton scatter**. For lower-energy photons, such as diagnostic imaging or kV therapy, the **photoelectric effect** becomes much more important. At higher photon energies, **pair production** can occur.

These interactions determine:

- how photons attenuate in tissue,
- how secondary electrons are produced,
- where dose is deposited,
- how shielding works,
- and why different beam energies behave differently clinically.

## Photoelectric Effect

In the **photoelectric effect**, an incident photon transfers all of its energy to a tightly bound orbital electron. The photon disappears, and the electron is ejected from the atom.

This interaction is more likely when:

- photon energy is low,
- the absorbing material has high atomic number,
- the electron is tightly bound.

### Clinical Meaning

Photoelectric effect is important for:

- diagnostic imaging contrast,
- kV imaging,
- bone and high-Z material interactions,
- shielding with high-Z materials such as lead.

It is less dominant for typical MV photon therapy beams.

---

## Compton Scatter

In **Compton scatter**, an incident photon interacts with a loosely bound outer-shell electron. The photon transfers part of its energy to the electron and continues in a new direction with reduced energy.

This is the dominant interaction for most **megavoltage external beam therapy**.

### Clinical Meaning

Compton scatter is important because it:

- produces secondary electrons that deposit dose,
- dominates interactions in soft tissue at MV energies,
- depends mainly on electron density,
- contributes to scatter dose outside the primary beam.

For treatment planning, this is why MV photon dose deposition is closely tied to tissue electron density.
