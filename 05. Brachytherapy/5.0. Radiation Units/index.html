    Radiation Units

    #Activity

    Activity (\(A\)) represents the number of particles (\(dN\)) which decay over time (\(dt\)) for a given radioactive source:
    $$
    \ Activity {\,} (A) = -\frac{dN}{dt}
    = \lambda N
    \tag{1}

    $$ where: - (A) = Activity - (dN) = number of particles to decay. - (dt) = over time

    Units of Activity are the following: $$
    \begin{aligned}
    1\,\mathrm{Bq} &= 1\, \mathrm{disintegration\, per\, second\, }(\mathrm{dps})\[4pt]
    1\,\mathrm{Ci} &= 3.7\times10^{10}\,\mathrm{Bq} \[4pt]
    1\,\mathrm{mg\,Ra\,eq} &= 8.25\,\mathrm{mCi} \[4pt]
    &= 3.05\times10^{8}\,\mathrm{Bq}
    \end{aligned}
    $$

    [!NOTE] The SI unit of activity is the becquerel (Bq). The curie (Ci) is still widely used in nuclear medicine and brachytherapy in the United States. Milligram radium equivalent (mg Ra eq) is a historical brachytherapy unit that has largely been replaced by air-kerma strength (SKS_KSK​) in modern dosimetry (TG-43).

    Source activity (\(A\)) decays as an exponential function of time (\(t\)) and the decay constant (\(\lambda\)), using Equation 2:

    \[ A(t)=A_0e^{-\lambda t} \tag{2} \]

    The derivation of Equation (2) from Equation (1) is shown below: $$
    \ \begin{aligned}
    A &= -\frac{dN}{dt} \
    \frac{dN}{dt} &= -\lambda N \
    \frac{dN}{N} &= -\lambda\,dt \
    \int \frac{1}{N}\,dN &= \int -\lambda\,dt \
    \ln N &= -\lambda t + C \
    N(t) &= N_0 e^{-\lambda t} \
    A &= \lambda N \
    A &= \lambda N_0 e^{-\lambda t} \
    A &= A_0 e^{-\lambda t}
    \end{aligned}
    \tag{3} \ $$

    #Exposure

    Exposure (\(X\)) describes the electric charge (\(dQ\)) per mass in dry air (\(dm\)):

    $$ \begin{aligned} X &= \frac{dQ}{dm} = \frac{\mathrm{C}}{\mathrm{kg}} \end{aligned} \tag{4} $$ where: - (X) = exposure - (dQ) = total charge of one sign produced in air - (dm) = mass of dry air

    Units of Exposure (X) are the following: $\(\begin{aligned}1\,\mathrm{C/kg} &= 3.876\times10^{3}\,\mathrm{R} \\[4pt]1\,\mathrm{R} &= 2.58\times10^{-4}\,\mathrm{C/kg} \\[4pt]\,\end{aligned}\)$

    To go from Activity (\(A\)) \(\rightarrow\) Exposure (\(X\)):

    \[ \\X = \frac{\Gamma A \, t}{r^2 } \tag{5} \\ $$ And exposure rate $\dot{X}$ can be defined as the following: $$ \dot{X}=\frac{\Gamma A}{r^2} \tag{6} \]

    where \(\Gamma\) is the exposure rate constant, with units of \(\frac{\mathrm{R}\cdot\mathrm{cm^2}}{\mathrm{mCi}\cdot\mathrm{hr}}\) or \(\frac{\mathrm{R}\cdot\mathrm{m^2}}{\mathrm{Ci}\cdot\mathrm{hr}}\).

    To go from Exposure (\(X\)) \(\rightarrow\) Air KERMA (\(K_{\mathrm{air}}\)):

    \[ K_{\mathrm{air}} = X\left(\frac{W}{e}\right) \tag{7} \]

    where: $$ \begin{aligned} X &= \frac{\mathrm{C}}{\mathrm{kg}} \[6pt] \frac{W}{e} &= 33.97\,\frac{\mathrm{J}}{\mathrm{C}} \[6pt] \end{aligned} $$ Substituting in units: $$ \begin{aligned} K_{\mathrm{air}} &= \left( \frac{\mathrm{C}}{\mathrm{kg}} \right) \left( \frac{\mathrm{J}}{\mathrm{C}} \right) \[6pt] &K_{\mathrm{air}}= \frac{\mathrm{J}}{\mathrm{kg}} \[6pt] &K_{\mathrm{air}}= \mathrm{Gy} \end{aligned} $$

    Finally, using Equation (5) and Equation (7), we are able to go from source activity (\(A\)) to air KERMA (\(K_{\mathrm{air}}\)).

    \[ \begin{aligned} \dot{K}_{\mathrm{air}} &= \dot{X}\left(\frac{W}{e}\right) \\[6pt] &= \dot{X} \left( 2.58\times10^{-4}\,\frac{\mathrm{C}}{\mathrm{kg}\cdot\mathrm{R}} \right) \left( 33.97\,\frac{\mathrm{J}}{\mathrm{C}} \right) \\[6pt] &= \dot{X} \left( 8.76\times10^{-3}\,\frac{\mathrm{J}}{\mathrm{kg}\cdot\mathrm{R}} \right) \\[6pt] &= \dot{X} \left( 8.76\times10^{-3}\,\frac{\mathrm{Gy}}{\mathrm{R}} \right) \\[6pt] &= \dot{X} \left( 0.876\,\frac{\mathrm{cGy}}{\mathrm{R}} \right) \\[6pt] &= 0.876\,\dot{X} \qquad (\mathrm{cGy/hr}) \end{aligned} \tag{8} $$ $$ \dot{K}= 0.876 \dot{X} \tag{9} \]

    #AirKermaStrength

    TG-43 using the convention of Air Kerma Strength (\(S_K\)) to describe the source strength, rather than activity.

    $$

    S_K = \dot{K} \times {d^2} \tag{10} $$ Where: $$
    \begin{aligned}
    \mathrm{Air \, Kerma \, Strength} = S_K
    &=
    \mathrm{U}
    =
    \mu\mathrm{Gy}\cdot\mathrm{m^2}/\mathrm{hr}
    =
    \mathrm{cGy}\cdot\mathrm{cm^2}/\mathrm{hr}
    \[6pt]
    \mathrm{Kerma \, Dose \, Rate} = \dot{K}_{\mathrm{air}}
    &=
    \frac{\mathrm{cGy}}{\mathrm{hr}}
    \[6pt]
    \mathrm{Distance \, from \, source} = d
    &=
    \mathrm{cm}
    \end{aligned}
    $$

    To calculate the total reference air kerma (TRAK):

    \[ TRAK = \sum S_k \times t \tag{11} \]

    To go from (\(S_k\)) \(\rightarrow\) Dose Rate (\(\dot{D}\)):

    $$

    \dot{D} = S_k \times \Lambda = \dot{K} \frac{\mu_{en}}{\rho} \tag{12} $$Where: $$ \begin{aligned} \mathrm{Dose \, Rate \, to \, Water} = \dot{D} &= \frac{\mathrm{cGy}}{\mathrm{hr}} \[6pt]

    \mathrm{Air \, Kerma \, Strength} = S_K &= \mathrm{U} = \mu\mathrm{Gy}\cdot\mathrm{m^2}/\mathrm{hr} = \mathrm{cGy}\cdot\mathrm{cm^2}/\mathrm{hr} \[6pt]

    \mathrm{Dose \, Rate \, Constant} = \Lambda &= \frac{\mathrm{cGy/hr}}{\mathrm{U}} \[6pt]

    \mathrm{Air \, Kerma \, Rate} = \dot{K}_{\mathrm{air}} &= \frac{\mathrm{cGy}}{\mathrm{hr}} \[6pt]

    \mathrm{Mass \, Energy\text{-}Absorption \, Coefficient}

    \frac{\mu_{\mathrm{en}}}{\rho} &= \frac{\mathrm{cm^2}}{\mathrm{g}} \end{aligned} $$

    Piecing it all together:

    Overarching connections to remember:

    $$
    \boxed{
    A
    \;\xrightarrow{\Gamma}\;
    \dot{X}
    \;\xrightarrow{W/e}\;
    \dot{K}{\mathrm{air}}
    \;\xrightarrow{\times d^2}\;
    S_K
    \;\xrightarrow{\Lambda}\;
    \dot{D}    }
    }

    $$

    1. We go from Activity (A) to exposure rate (\(\dot{X}\)), using Equation (6). $$
      \dot{X}=\frac{\Gamma A}{r^2}
      \tag{6}
      $$
    2. We go from Exposure rate (\(\dot{X}\)) to Kerma Rate (\(\dot{K}\)) in Equation (9). $$
      \dot{K}= 0.876 \dot{X} \tag{9}
      $$
    3. We go from Kerma Rate (\(\dot{K}\)) to Air Kerma Strength (\(S_k\)), using Equation (10). $$

    S_K = \dot{K} \times {d^2} \tag{10} $$ 4. We go from Air Kerma Strength (\(S_k\)) to Dose Rate, using Equation (12).

    $$

    \dot{D} = S_k \times \Lambda = \dot{K} \frac{\mu_{en}}{\rho} \tag{12} $$
